25         Petitioner Sachin Shah seeks review of a January 31, 2023 final decision by

26   the Administrative Review Board (“ARB”) of the United States Department of

27   Labor affirming an administrative law judge’s (“ALJ”) dismissal of Shah’s

28   whistleblower retaliation claims under the Sarbanes-Oxley Act (”SOX”), 18 U.S.C.

                                              2
 1   § 1514A. Shah alleges that he was unlawfully fired by Intervenor TD Securities

 2   (USA) LLC after reporting securities violations at his former employer Albert Fried

 3   & Co., which TD Securities acquired. We assume the parties’ familiarity with the

 4   underlying facts and the record of prior proceedings, to which we refer only as

5    necessary to explain our decision to DENY the petition. 1

6          We will affirm the ARB’s decision unless, among other reasons, it is

7    “arbitrary, capricious, an abuse of discretion, or otherwise not in accordance with

8    law,” or is “unsupported by substantial evidence.” 5 U.S.C. § 706(2)(A), (E); Bechtel

 9   v. Admin. Rev. Bd., 710 F.3d 443, 445–46 (2d Cir. 2013). On appeal, Shah argues the

10   ARB’s conclusion—that there was no genuine issue of material fact that an

11   affirmative defense barred his claims—was unsupported by the evidence. 2 We

12   disagree.

 1   a preponderance of the evidence that: “(1) he . . . engaged in a protected activity;

2    (2) the employer knew that he . . . engaged in the protected activity; (3) he . . .

3    suffered an unfavorable personnel action; and (4) the protected activity was a

4    contributing factor in the unfavorable action.” Bechtel, 710 F.3d at 451. If an

5    employee succeeds in making out a prima facie case, the burden shifts to the

 6   employer, who can avoid liability if it proves by “clear and convincing evidence

 7   that it would have taken the same unfavorable personnel action in the absence of

 8   the protected behavior.” Id.; 18 U.S.C. § 1514A(a); 49 U.S.C. § 42121(b)(2)(B)(iv);

 9   29 C.F.R. § 1980.109(b). Summary decision on a SOX claim is appropriate where

10   “there is no genuine dispute as to any material fact” and the moving party is

11   entitled to judgment as a matter of law. 29 C.F.R. § 18.72(a).

12         Even assuming Shah established a prima facie case, we agree with the ARB

13   that TD Securities established by unrebutted, clear and convincing evidence that

14   it would have terminated Shah regardless of any protected activity he took.

15   Substantial evidence showed that TD Securities determined it had no need for

16   Shah’s position long before Shah contacted the relevant authorities to report

17   securities violations. Another Albert Fried employee was terminated for the same

18   reason. Murray v. U.B.S. Sec., LLC, 601 U.S. 23, 38–39 (2024) (explaining that the

                                              4
 1   relevant question is whether the employer would have retained an otherwise

 2   identical employee who did not engage in protected activity). Shah points to no

 3   evidence to the contrary, only alleging—without support—that the affidavits

 4   submitted by TD Securities and Alfred Fried were false; TD Securities, Alfred

 5   Fried, Shah’s attorneys, and others engaged in a conspiracy to stalk and harass

 6   him; and TD Securities recently terminated several employees to cover up its

 7   securities violations. Because Shah failed to show a dispute of fact to the contrary,

 8   29 C.F.R. § 18.72(c)(1), summary decision was warranted. 3

9          We have considered the rest of Shah’s arguments and conclude they are

10   without merit. Accordingly, we DENY the petition for review.
